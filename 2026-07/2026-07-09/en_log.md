The most important thing in building trust is the ability to see what the other person truly needs. I've worked as a sales partner for many years, and I've come to realize that a good partner doesn't just listen to what the customer says. They figure out what the customer is actually struggling with, and what they really need right now.

For example, imagine it starts raining and a client asks you to bring an umbrella. Even if you bring one, what the client is really worried about isn't the umbrella itself. It's that they don't want to get wet. So instead of just handing them an umbrella, you could offer to call a car, or guide them through a covered route. In other words, you don't act on what they say. You act on what they actually need.

People who can do this gradually earn deeper trust. What they have in common is that they genuinely try to understand the other person. Whether it's sales or any other situation, when you're talking with someone, you should always ask yourself, "What is this person really trying to say?" You put yourself in their shoes and truly try to understand them.

Because what someone says isn't always what they really mean. To build trust, you have to meet the other person's expectations, and to do that, you first have to understand what they truly expect.

So for me, the key to trust is this: understand the other person's real needs, act on them, and always keep the attitude of truly trying to understand the other side. Don't just listen. Stand in their position, see things from their point of view, and really try to understand them. That, I believe, is the most important thing in building trust.
